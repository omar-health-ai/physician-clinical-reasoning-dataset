# Clinical Reasoning Framework v1.0

## Purpose

This framework defines the reasoning structure used throughout the Physician Clinical Reasoning Dataset (PCRD).

The goal is to demonstrate how physicians approach clinical problems using structured reasoning, evidence-based medicine, patient safety principles, and guideline-informed decision-making.

---

## 1. Clinical Problem Representation

Start by summarizing the patient in one concise sentence.

A strong problem representation includes:

- Age
- Sex
- Main symptom
- Duration
- Important risk factors
- Key abnormal findings
- Clinical context

### Example

A 39-year-old male smoker with diabetes presents with acute epigastric/chest pain, vomiting, and diaphoresis, raising concern for acute coronary syndrome.

---

## 2. Immediate Safety Assessment

Before making a final diagnosis, identify life-threatening conditions that must not be missed.

Ask:

- Is the patient unstable?
- Are there airway, breathing, or circulation problems?
- Are there red flags?
- Does the presentation require immediate intervention?
- Is this an emergency diagnosis until proven otherwise?

---

## 3. Differential Diagnosis

Build a prioritized differential diagnosis.

The differential should include:

1. Most likely diagnosis
2. Most dangerous diagnosis
3. Common alternatives
4. Less common but important diagnoses

### Differential Diagnosis Categories

- Cardiovascular
- Respiratory
- Neurological
- Gastrointestinal
- Endocrine/metabolic
- Infectious
- Renal/urological
- Hematological
- Toxicological
- Psychiatric or functional, only after dangerous causes are excluded

---

## 4. Red Flags

Identify findings that increase urgency or change management.

Examples:

- Hypotension
- Altered mental status
- Severe chest pain
- Syncope
- Respiratory distress
- Focal neurological deficit
- Sepsis features
- Persistent vomiting
- Severe dehydration
- Immunocompromised status
- Pregnancy
- Extremes of age

---

## 5. Focused History

Use the history to refine probability.

Important components:

- Onset
- Duration
- Character
- Location
- Radiation
- Associated symptoms
- Aggravating and relieving factors
- Past medical history
- Medication history
- Allergies
- Family history
- Social history
- Risk factors
- Relevant exposures

---

## 6. Focused Physical Examination

The physical examination should test the leading hypotheses.

Include:

- General appearance
- Vital signs
- Cardiovascular examination
- Respiratory examination
- Abdominal examination
- Neurological examination
- Skin and extremities
- Hydration status
- Signs of shock or sepsis

---

## 7. Initial Investigations

Investigations should be selected based on clinical reasoning, not ordered randomly.

Each investigation should answer a clinical question.

### Examples

- ECG: Is there ischemia, arrhythmia, or conduction abnormality?
- Troponin: Is there myocardial injury?
- CBC: Is there infection, anemia, or thrombocytopenia?
- Electrolytes/renal function: Is there metabolic derangement or kidney injury?
- Chest X-ray: Is there pneumonia, pneumothorax, pulmonary edema, or widened mediastinum?
- CT scan: Is there a dangerous structural diagnosis requiring urgent confirmation?

---

## 8. Clinical Reasoning Explanation

This is the core of the case.

Explain:

- Why the leading diagnosis is most likely
- Why alternative diagnoses are less likely
- Which findings support the diagnosis
- Which findings argue against other diagnoses
- What information is still missing
- What must be done immediately

---

## 9. Evidence-Based Management

Management should be organized by urgency.

### Immediate Management

Actions required now to stabilize the patient or prevent harm.

### Diagnostic Management

Tests required to confirm or exclude important diagnoses.

### Definitive Management

Disease-specific treatment based on best available evidence.

### Disposition

Decide whether the patient needs:

- Discharge
- Observation
- Admission
- ICU care
- Emergency procedure
- Specialist consultation

---

## 10. Guideline Alignment

Each case should reference relevant clinical guidelines when appropriate.

Examples:

- AHA
- ESC
- NICE
- WHO
- IDSA
- KDIGO
- Surviving Sepsis Campaign
- CTAS
- ACLS
- ATLS
- PALS

The goal is not to copy guidelines, but to demonstrate guideline-informed clinical reasoning.

---

## 11. Gold Standard Physician Answer

The gold standard answer should be:

- Clinically accurate
- Concise but complete
- Safe
- Evidence-based
- Structured
- Appropriate to the clinical setting
- Clear about uncertainty
- Clear about urgency

---

## 12. Common AI Errors

Each case should identify mistakes that an AI model might make.

Examples:

- Missing a life-threatening diagnosis
- Anchoring too early
- Recommending unsafe outpatient management
- Ignoring abnormal vital signs
- Giving non-specific advice
- Over-ordering unnecessary tests
- Under-ordering essential tests
- Failing to mention red flags
- Giving treatment without diagnosis
- Not following current guidelines

---

## 13. Teaching Notes

Each case should include educational points for learners.

Teaching notes should answer:

- What is the key lesson?
- What makes this case dangerous?
- What is commonly missed?
- How should the diagnosis be approached?
- What should the learner remember?

---

## 14. Final Case Quality Checklist

Before publishing a case, confirm:

- The case has a clear clinical problem.
- The differential diagnosis is prioritized.
- Life-threatening diagnoses are addressed.
- Red flags are identified.
- Investigations are justified.
- Management is evidence-based.
- The gold standard answer is safe and complete.
- Common AI errors are included.
- Teaching points are clear.
- The case is suitable for medical education and Healthcare AI evaluation.
